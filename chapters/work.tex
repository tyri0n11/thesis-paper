This chapter delves into the existing literature and technological advancements that form the foundation of the UrbanPulse platform. It evaluates current methodologies in real-time urban monitoring, analyzes the architectural shift toward Data Lakehouses, and identifies the research gaps that necessitate a dual-signal anomaly detection framework.

\section{Current Advancements}
Traditional batch processing systems, such as those built on \textbf{Apache Spark}~\cite{spark_streaming} or Hadoop-era ETL pipelines~\cite{kleppmann_ddia}, excel at high-throughput historical analysis but introduce latency on the order of hours, making them unsuitable for time-critical urban incident detection. The \textbf{Lambda Architecture}~\cite{kleppmann_ddia} addressed real-time needs by running a parallel speed layer, yet at the cost of duplicated processing logic and operational complexity. The \textbf{Kappa Architecture}~\cite{kafka-definitive-guide} simplified this by eliminating the batch layer entirely, unifying all processing through a single streaming pipeline using systems such as \textbf{Apache Kafka}~\cite{kafka-definitive-guide} and \textbf{Redpanda}~\cite{redpanda-streaming}. However, Kappa systems typically lack native support for schema evolution and transactional data versioning, which are essential for long-lived urban datasets. The emergence of the \textbf{Medallion Lakehouse architecture}~\cite{databricks_lakehouse}, powered by table formats such as \textbf{Apache Iceberg}~\cite{iceberg-table-format}, offers transactional integrity and schema evolution alongside a structured Bronze–Silver–Gold data lineage. In the domain of anomaly detection, state-of-the-art approaches~\cite{chandola2009anomaly} leverage a combination of \textbf{statistical baselines} and \textbf{unsupervised learning} — such as Isolation Forest~\cite{isolation-forest} — to handle the high dimensionality of city-scale telemetry, but these approaches have primarily been evaluated on offline, static benchmark datasets~\cite{chandola2009anomaly}.
\section{Research Gap}
Despite these advancements, a significant \textit{semantic gap} persists between raw telemetry ingestion and actionable intelligence. Most existing systems process traffic data in isolated batches, leading to high latency in incident response. There is a lack of integrated frameworks that ensure \textbf{stream-to-batch consistency}, where real-time anomaly signals are validated against historical neighborhood rhythms stored in a Lakehouse. In our own experiments, systems that rely solely on static thresholds exhibit elevated False Positive Rates under low-variance, off-peak traffic conditions — a limitation we term the \textit{cold-start} problem. Critically, existing systems often treat anomaly detection as a \textit{static} problem, whereas UrbanPulse treats it as a \textbf{dynamic evolution}, leveraging \textbf{Welford's online algorithm} for continuous baseline updates without heavy recomputation. UrbanPulse addresses these gaps by implementing a unified \textbf{dual-signal detector} that reconciles instant Z-score deviations with historical machine learning inference.

\subsection{The Medallion Lakehouse Architecture}
The core data strategy of this thesis follows the Medallion pattern, organized into three distinct layers: \textbf{Bronze} (raw ingestion), \textbf{Silver} (filtered and cleaned), and \textbf{Gold} (aggregated business-level features). \subsubsection{Event-Driven Hybrid Stream-Batch Architecture}
Unlike traditional batch ETL pipelines~\cite{kleppmann_ddia}, which accumulate data in periodic jobs and introduce multi-hour latency, UrbanPulse employs an \textbf{event-driven, hybrid stream-batch architecture}. \textbf{Redpanda} (Kafka-compatible) acts as the central event bus, decoupling producers from consumers and enabling two independent processing tiers to operate on the same event stream. The \textbf{speed layer} consumes each Kafka message immediately: the online service applies Welford's online algorithm~\cite{welford1962} per-event to maintain a live statistical baseline and writes anomaly flags to PostgreSQL within seconds. Concurrently, the \textbf{batch layer} — orchestrated by \textbf{Prefect}~\cite{prefect_docs} — runs scheduled micro-batch and hourly jobs to promote data through the Bronze~$\rightarrow$~Silver~$\rightarrow$~Gold medallion pipeline and retrain the Isolation Forest model. A unified serving API merges both tiers at query time, delivering near-real-time anomaly results enriched with historical context. Incremental updates are then pushed to clients via \textbf{Server-Sent Events (SSE)}~\cite{sse_spec}. \subsection{Anomaly Detection Logic}
UrbanPulse utilizes a dual-signal framework to identify traffic incidents. The statistical signal is calculated using the Z-score formula to detect immediate spikes in congestion ratio $r$ relative to the moving average $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$:

\begin{equation}
    Z = \frac{r - \mu}{\sigma}
\end{equation}

Simultaneously, an \textbf{Isolation Forest}~\cite{isolation-forest} model operates on a \textbf{7-dimensional feature vector} — four cyclical temporal encodings ($\sin$/$\cos$ of hour of day and day of week) combined with three traffic ratio features — to identify non-recurrent patterns that simple statistics might miss. The model is formalized as:

\begin{align}
	f &: \mathbb{R}^7 \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \\
	\mathbf{x} &= [\sin_h,\ \cos_h,\ \sin_{dow},\ \cos_{dow},\ r_{heavy},\ r_{moderate},\ n_{severe}]
\end{align}

where $f(\mathbf{x})$ is the anomaly score returned by the Isolation Forest's \texttt{decision\_function}~\cite{isolation-forest}. The score partitions the real line into two decision regions:
\begin{equation}
	\text{label}(\mathbf{x}) =
	\begin{cases}
		\text{anomaly} & \text{if } f(\mathbf{x}) < 0 \quad (s \in [-1,\ 0)) \\
		\text{normal}  & \text{if } f(\mathbf{x}) \geq 0 \quad (s \in [0,\ +1])
	\end{cases}
\end{equation}
A score approaching $-1$ indicates a strong anomaly, while scores near $+1$ correspond to densely clustered, normal traffic observations.
